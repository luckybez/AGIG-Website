About the founder

Travis Lewis

A career spent building systems for people operating under consequence. First technical infrastructure. Now cognitive infrastructure.

Built for consequence

Travis was drawn early to problems where the answer had to work, not merely sound convincing.

After studying at Choate Rosemary Hall and the University of Pennsylvania, he built a career supporting mission-critical technology environments, including trading desks where failure was expensive, time mattered, and technology existed to serve the outcome.

The deeper work was always translation: understand what the person or business needed, turn that need into architecture, and build a system they could rely on under pressure.

From infrastructure to decision systems

That work eventually led him into entrepreneurship, computational systems, crypto mining, and algorithmic trading.

While building and operating those systems, he encountered a recurring problem: a sound decision process could still be weakened by emotion, inconsistency, and incomplete context.

He began encoding his trading principles into software. The lesson was larger than trading. Judgment becomes more consistent when its principles, assumptions, and boundaries are made explicit and built into a system.

When modern AI emerged, Travis recognized that it could do more than generate content or automate tasks. It could help examine reasoning, surface blind spots, preserve context, and bring relevant perspectives into a decision.

He committed himself to understanding the capability fully: how it worked, where it failed, how it should be governed, and how it could strengthen people without replacing them.

When the stakes became personal

The idea became real during a consequential eye-surgery decision involving several possible procedures and specialists who did not agree.

The relevant information was scattered across consultations, imaging, clinical research, risks, assumptions, and conflicting recommendations. No single conversation held the entire decision.

Travis built a private system to bring it together. It compared the options, separated evidence from assumption, surfaced unanswered questions, and prepared the tradeoffs for discussion with his doctors.

The system did not make the decision. It helped him arrive prepared to make it.

What began as support for one consequential choice became a private system around his work and life: preserving important history, pressure-testing assumptions, bringing specialist perspectives forward, and strengthening judgment across the decisions that followed. Read the founder case →

The larger idea

Only later did Travis understand that the ambition behind the system was not new.

For more than a century, thinkers including Paul Otlet, H. G. Wells, Vannevar Bush, J. C. R. Licklider, and Douglas Engelbart had explored ways to extend human memory, organize knowledge, and augment human intellect.

Their work revealed a long-standing pursuit: not machines that replace human judgment, but systems that help people understand more, remember more, and decide better.

Modern AI did not create that ambition. It made a more complete expression of it possible. Meet the thinkers →

Why Avant Garde exists

Avant Garde grew from a lifetime of building infrastructure for people operating under pressure.

The burden has changed. First it was technical. Now it is cognitive.

Avant Garde builds private cognitive infrastructure around people who remain accountable for consequential decisions. Founders, principals, and leaders who must hold the history, commitments, risks, competing priorities, and human consequences behind the call.

The system supports their judgment. Responsibility remains with them.

The common thread

Eight generations asked the same question and hit the same wall. Judgment fails for lack of support, not lack of intelligence, and in every era, the technology could not yet build the system they could see. The idea was never wrong. The infrastructure was never ready. Now it is.

Otto’s notebook · 1998

Philosophers Andy Clark and David Chalmers imagined a man named Otto, whose memory was failing. Otto carried a notebook everywhere and wrote down everything that mattered. When he wanted to reach a museum across town, he didn’t strain to remember the address. He opened the book and walked. Their conclusion changed the philosophy of mind: the notebook wasn’t a tool Otto used. It was part of his memory, because it was always with him, instantly at hand, and trusted without a second thought.

That is the standard an Avant Garde system is built to earn.

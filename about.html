About the founder

Travis Lewis

A career spent building systems for people operating under consequence. First technical infrastructure. Now cognitive infrastructure.

Built for consequence

Travis was drawn early to problems where the answer had to work, not merely sound convincing.

After studying at Choate Rosemary Hall and the University of Pennsylvania, he built a career supporting mission-critical technology environments, including trading desks where failure was expensive, time mattered, and technology existed to serve the outcome.

The deeper work was always translation: understand what the person or business needed, turn that need into architecture, and build a system they could rely on under pressure.

From infrastructure to decision systems

That work eventually led him into entrepreneurship, computational systems, crypto mining, and algorithmic trading.

While building and operating those systems, he encountered a recurring problem: a sound decision process could still be weakened by emotion, inconsistency, and incomplete context.

He began encoding his trading principles into software. The lesson was larger than trading. Judgment becomes more consistent when its principles, assumptions, and boundaries are made explicit and built into a system.

When modern AI emerged, Travis recognized that it could do more than generate content or automate tasks. It could help examine reasoning, surface blind spots, preserve context, and bring relevant perspectives into a decision. He committed himself to understanding the capability fully: how it worked, where it failed, how it should be governed, and how it could strengthen people without replacing them.

When the stakes became personal

The idea became real during a consequential eye-surgery decision involving several possible procedures and specialists who did not agree.

The relevant information was scattered across consultations, imaging, clinical research, risks, assumptions, and conflicting recommendations. No single conversation held the entire decision.

Travis built a private system to bring it together. It compared the options, separated evidence from assumption, surfaced unanswered questions, and prepared the tradeoffs for discussion with his doctors.

The system did not make the decision. It helped him arrive prepared to make it.

What began as support for one consequential choice became a private system around his work and life: preserving important history, pressure-testing assumptions, bringing specialist perspectives forward, and strengthening judgment across the decisions that followed.

Why Avant Garde exists

Avant Garde grew from a lifetime of building infrastructure for people operating under pressure. The burden has changed. First it was technical. Now it is cognitive.

Avant Garde builds private cognitive infrastructure around people who remain accountable for consequential decisions — founders, principals, and leaders who must hold the history, commitments, risks, competing priorities, and human consequences behind the call.

The system supports their judgment. Responsibility remains with them.

“My work is to build the infrastructure behind people whose decisions can create meaningful good.”

The lineage

This was always the project.

For more than a century, serious thinkers pursued the same idea: systems that extend human intellect without replacing human judgment. Each hit the same wall. Open any name to see what they asked, where it led, and what stopped them.

1840William WhewellConsilience

The question he asked

When independent lines of evidence converge on one conclusion, how should a mind hold them together?

Where it led

He named it consilience — in the same book that coined the word "scientist." Darwin made it his working method.

What blocked him

There was no machinery to hold converging knowledge together. Consilience remained a discipline of one mind at a time.

1910Paul OtletThe Mundaneum

The question he asked

Could the world's knowledge be organized so that anyone could ask a question and receive the relevant answer?

Where it led

The Mundaneum: millions of index cards, a universal classification, and a knowledge service by mail and telegraph.

What blocked him

Paper. The connections between cards had to be maintained by hand, and the system could not move at the speed of thought.

1938H. G. WellsWorld Brain

The question he asked

Could civilization share one living, continuously revised body of knowledge?

Where it led

The World Brain essays: a permanent world encyclopedia as the foundation for better collective decisions.

What blocked him

Microfilm could store knowledge, but it could not keep knowledge alive — updating, connecting, and answering.

1945Vannevar BushThe Memex

The question he asked

How can one person command the growing record of what they know, promised, and learned?

Where it led

The Memex: a private desk that stored everything and linked it by association — trails of reasoning a person could revisit.

What blocked him

It could not be built. The hardware of his era could never deliver the desk he could describe.

1960J. C. R. LickliderMan-Computer Symbiosis

The question he asked

What if people and machines thought together in real time — each doing what it does best?

Where it led

Man-Computer Symbiosis — and the funding decisions that seeded the research networks that became the internet.

What blocked him

Computing was too scarce and too shared. The symbiosis could not yet belong to one person's judgment.

1962Douglas EngelbartAugmenting Human Intellect

The question he asked

Could technology raise a person's capability to understand and resolve complex problems — not replace it?

Where it led

Augmenting Human Intellect, and the 1968 demonstration that shaped modern computing.

What blocked him

The tools shipped everywhere. The augmentation framework — the system around judgment — was left behind.

1998Clark & ChalmersThe Extended Mind

The question they asked

Where does the mind end? If a tool is always with you, instantly at hand, and trusted — is it part of your memory?

Where it led

The Extended Mind — and Otto's notebook, the philosophical standard for trusted external memory.

What blocked them

Nothing yet could earn that trust at the level of judgment — holding not just facts, but reasoning, commitments, and risk.

NowThe infrastructure is ready.

The common thread

Eight generations asked the same question and hit the same wall. Judgment fails for lack of support, not lack of intelligence — and in every era, the technology could not yet build the system they could see. The idea was never wrong. The infrastructure was never ready. Now it is.

Otto's notebook · 1998

Philosophers Andy Clark and David Chalmers imagined a man named Otto, whose memory was failing. Otto carried a notebook everywhere and wrote down everything that mattered. When he wanted to reach a museum across town, he didn't strain to remember the address. He opened the book and walked.

Their conclusion changed the philosophy of mind: the notebook wasn't a tool Otto used. It was part of his memory, because it was always with him, instantly at hand, and trusted without a second thought.

That is the standard an Avant Garde system is built to earn.
